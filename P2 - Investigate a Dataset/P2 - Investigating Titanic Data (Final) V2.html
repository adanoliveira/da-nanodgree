A Jouney into Titanic's passengers data¶

Introduction¶

In this notebook, I'll analyze a dataset containing demographics and passenger information from 891 of the 2224 passengers and crew on board the Titanic. Although there was some element of luck involved in surviving the sinking, we can assume that some groups of people were more likely to survive than others.

In this analysis, I'll look for patterns that describe how the population who survived the sinking differed from the population who didn't. Based on what we know about the accident, that women and children were prioritized and probably class influenced who were evacuated first, here are the quesitons I'm going to explore:

Exploratory Questions¶

  1. How does survival rate change by class? Are wealthier individuals (1st class) more likely to survive than the poor (3rd class)?
  2. How does survival rate change by gender? Does gender affect the chances of surviving?
  3. How does age impact survival rates? Are older people more likely to die?

This samples's survival rate is 40.45%, higher than the official survival rate() of 32.2%. Our dataset contains a greater proportion of survivers maybe because (we can assume that) it's easier to collect data from them than from the passangers who didn't make it.   • 13.3% were young adults (from 12 to 19 years old)
  • 73.5% were adults (from 20 to 59 years old)

Only 43.5% of passangers were accompained by family.

The mean fare price is 34.56, but more than 75% of the passangers paid less than that.

63.6% of the passangers in the sample are males and 36.4%, females.

Observations¶

From the charts, we can notice some insteresting trends.

Appart from the ones already meantioned, such as survival rate and the prevalence of men aboard, we can see that:

  • the 3rd class is the one with the highest number of people (355), followed by the 1st class (184) and lastly by the 2nd class (173);
  • age distribution changes significantly by class. The 1st class has a greter share of older people, while the 3rd class is the youngest. The 2nd class distribution sits somewhere in the middle of the other two, but it's more similar to 3rd class' distribution;
  • most people were boarded in Southampton (554), with the second largest group in Cherbourg (130) and the smallest group in Queenstown (28);
  • women aboard were slighly younger than men;
  • fares on average are the lowest for the 3rd class and the highest for the 1st class, as expected. But it's interesting that there's a relevant overlap of fare values for the 3 classes;
  • most passengers travelled alone, 56.5% of them;
  • for the passangers travelling with families, the 2nd and 3rd class passengers seem to have travelled more in couples than the 3rd class ones, who seem to have travelled in bigger families, with more children and simblings.

Conclusion¶

With the t-Tests calculated above, we can conclude that:

  • For men, the differences in survival rate from the 1st class to the 2nd and 3rd classes are significant. But the difference between the 2nd and the 3rd isn't significant. So we can say that for men, being from the 1st class increased chances of survival when compared to being from the 2nd or 3rd classes, which had about the same survival rate.
  • For women, the differences in survival rate from the 3rd class to the 1st and 2nd classes are significant. But the difference between the 1st and the 2nd isn't significant. So, for women, being from either the 1st or 2nd class didn't change the chance of survival significantly, but being from the 3rd class made them half as likely to survive.

  • Old = more than 60 years old

Conclusion¶

From the statistical tests results above, we can notice an interesting fact: though we observe decreasing survival rates for older groups, most of these differences aren't significant at a confidence level of 95%. But here's what we can conclude with 95% confidence:

  • babies and children, in other words, passanger up to 12 years old were more likely to survive than adults and old people (more than 20 years old);
  • babies and children weren't siginificantly more likely to survive than young passengers (from 13 to 19 years old);
  • we didn't observe significant survival rate differences among young, adult and old passangers (all those older than 12 years old).
